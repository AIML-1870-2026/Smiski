// About this tool
How to Read This Data

This tool queries the FDA Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) — voluntary reports submitted when a drug is suspected of causing a problem.

⚠ More reports ≠ more dangerous. A drug with 500,000 reports is likely just prescribed to millions of people. Always compare rates, not raw counts.

What this tool can tell you

  • Which adverse reactions are most frequently reported
  • How serious reported events tend to be (hospitalization, death rates)
  • How a drug's safety profile compares to another
  • Whether a drug has been recalled and why
  • What the official FDA label says about warnings and interactions

What this tool cannot tell you

  • Whether a drug caused a reaction — correlations only
  • Your personal risk — individual factors matter enormously
  • Whether a drug is safe or unsafe for you specifically
// Data source
About FAERS Reports

FAERS is the FDA Adverse Event Reporting System. Reports are submitted by patients, doctors, pharmacists, and manufacturers.

Critical limitations

  • Voluntary reporting — only a fraction of real events are reported
  • No denominator — we don't know how many people took the drug, so true incidence rates can't be calculated
  • Reporting bias — newer and heavily marketed drugs get more reports
  • Correlation, not causation — a patient may have been taking 10 drugs at once
The FDA uses FAERS as a signal detection tool — to flag patterns worth investigating, not to prove a drug is dangerous.
// Drug interactions
Known Dangerous Drug Pairs

These classic combinations are well-documented as dangerous. High co-admin report counts for these pairs in FAERS can reflect real interaction signals.

Warfarin + NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen)
Risk: Serious bleeding. NSAIDs reduce platelet function and can increase warfarin's anticoagulant effect dramatically.
MAO Inhibitors + Serotonergic drugs (SSRIs, tramadol)
Risk: Serotonin syndrome — potentially fatal agitation, rapid heart rate, high temperature, and seizures.
Methotrexate + NSAIDs
Risk: Methotrexate toxicity. NSAIDs reduce kidney clearance causing dangerous drug build-up.
Statins + CYP3A4 inhibitors (clarithromycin)
Risk: Rhabdomyolysis — muscle breakdown that can cause kidney failure.
ACE Inhibitors + Potassium-sparing diuretics
Risk: Dangerously high potassium levels, which can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmias.

Try the Co-Admin tab to see FAERS report counts for any drug pair.

// Adverse reactions
Understanding Reported Reactions

Reactions are coded using MedDRA — a standardized medical dictionary used globally by regulatory agencies.

Why some reactions look unexpected

  • Drug ineffective — patient didn't respond to treatment
  • Off-label use — drug used for unapproved purpose
  • Death — this is an outcome, not a reaction itself

High counts don't mean high risk

A reaction appearing 10,000 times for a drug with 2 million reports is quite rare. Consider counts relative to total reports shown in Overview.

Top 3 reactions (amber) are most frequent — frequency alone doesn't indicate severity.
// Outcomes
How to Interpret Outcomes

Each FAERS report includes an outcome — what happened to the patient. These are self-reported at time of filing.

Outcome categories

  • Recovered — patient fully recovered
  • Recovering — still recovering at time of report
  • Not recovered — condition persisted
  • Fatal — patient died (may or may not be drug-related)
  • Unknown — outcome not provided
A high fatal percentage may mean the drug is prescribed to critically ill patients — not that the drug itself is particularly deadly.
// Drug recalls
Understanding Recall Classifications
  • Class I — Most serious. Reasonable probability of serious harm or death. Examples: contaminated injectables, wrong dosage strength.
  • Class II — Moderate. May cause temporary adverse consequences; serious consequences are remote.
  • Class III — Least serious. Unlikely to cause health consequences but violates FDA regulations.

Why some drugs show no recalls

No results usually means no recent recalls, or the drug name differs in the enforcement database. Recalls are product-specific — brand names and lot numbers matter more here than generic names.

// Drug labels
What Drug Labels Actually Tell You

The FDA-approved label ("prescribing information") is the most authoritative source of drug information — reviewed and approved by the FDA before a drug goes to market.

Key sections

  • Warnings — Black box warnings are the most serious and legally required
  • Indications — What the drug is officially approved to treat
  • Contraindications — Who absolutely should not take this drug
  • Adverse Reactions — Side effects observed in clinical trials
  • Drug Interactions — Known interactions with other medications
Unlike FAERS reports, label data is carefully reviewed and verified by the FDA before approval.
// Co-administration
Analyzing Drug Co-Administration

This tab queries FAERS for reports where both drugs appear in the same report — meaning both were taken by the same patient at the same time.

What high co-admin counts might mean

  • Common co-prescription — the drugs are routinely prescribed together
  • Interaction signal — the combination generates more reports than expected
  • Overlapping patient populations — both drugs treat conditions common in the same group
Co-administration data alone cannot identify a drug interaction. For confirmed interactions, check the Label Info tab or a clinical source like Lexicomp.
// Drug class explorer
Exploring Drug Classes

This mode lets you compare multiple drugs within the same therapeutic class — medications that work similarly and treat the same conditions.

Why compare within a class?

Drugs in the same class often have similar mechanisms but different safety profiles. For example, all statins lower cholesterol, but some may have more muscle-related adverse event reports than others.

How to use it

  • Select a drug class (e.g. "Statins")
  • Toggle individual drugs on/off to narrow the comparison (minimum 2)
  • Click "Compare Class" to load data for all selected drugs simultaneously
  • The leaderboard ranks drugs by serious event rate within the class
Rankings reflect reporting rates, not proven safety differences. Always discuss medication choices with a doctor.
